## Category 1: Directly Related -- Politicians' Wealth, Local Governance, and Fiscal Policy

### 1. Fisman, Schulz, and Vig (2014) -- "The Private Returns to Public Office"
**Journal:** Journal of Political Economy, 122(4), 806-862.
**Relevance:** HIGH | **Proximity:** 5

Using public asset disclosures required of all candidates in Indian state elections, the authors estimate private financial returns to holding office. A regression discontinuity design comparing close election winners vs. losers shows that annual asset growth of winners is 3-5 percentage points higher than that of runners-up. The differential is larger in more corrupt states and for those holding ministerial positions. This paper establishes the key data infrastructure (mandatory asset declarations) and identification strategy (close-election RDD) that is directly analogous to the Brazilian setting with TSE data.

- **Identification:** RDD on close elections (winner vs. runner-up)
- **Data:** Mandatory candidate asset disclosures, Indian state elections
- **Main result:** Winners' assets grow 3-5 pp/year faster than losers' (+, large)
- **Key for us:** Demonstrates feasibility of using asset declarations + RDD for politician wealth questions

### 2. Szakonyi (2018) -- "Businesspeople in Elected Office: Identifying Private Benefits from Firm-Level Returns"
**Journal:** American Political Science Review, 112(2), 322-338.
**Relevance:** HIGH | **Proximity:** 5

Uses an original dataset of 2,703 firms in Russia and a regression discontinuity design to identify the causal effect of firm directors winning seats in subnational legislatures (2004-2013). Having a connection to a winning politician increases firm revenue by 60% and profitability by 15% over a term in office. Connected firms improve performance by gaining access to bureaucrats, not by signaling legitimacy to financiers. This directly speaks to the self-dealing mechanism: politicians in local office benefit their own business assets.

- **Identification:** RDD on close elections for subnational legislature seats
- **Data:** Matched firm-politician data, Russian subnational elections
- **Main result:** Firm revenue +60%, profitability +15% for winning politicians' firms (+, very large)
- **Key for us:** Closest methodological analog -- politician's own economic assets and local policy

### 3. Szakonyi (2020) -- "Politics for Profit: Business, Elections, and Policymaking in Russia"
**Publisher:** Cambridge University Press.
**Relevance:** HIGH | **Proximity:** 4

Book-length treatment using RDD on 33,000+ Russian mayors and legislators. Businessperson politicians increase expenditures on roads and transport while leaving health and education spending untouched. They do not reduce budget deficits but adopt less competitive methods for selecting contractors, particularly in corruption-ripe construction. Provides comprehensive theory and evidence on how politicians with business assets shape fiscal policy to benefit themselves.

- **Identification:** RDD on close elections, large-N
- **Data:** Russian mayoral and legislative elections, fiscal data
- **Main result:** Business politicians shift spending composition toward infrastructure (+, moderate)
- **Key for us:** Directly addresses how politician wealth/business interests shape fiscal policy

### 4. Eggers and Hainmueller (2009) -- "MPs for Sale? Returns to Office in Postwar British Politics"
**Journal:** American Political Science Review, 103(4), 513-533.
**Relevance:** HIGH | **Proximity:** 4

Using original data on estates of recently deceased British politicians and a regression discontinuity design comparing MPs with parliamentary candidates who narrowly lost, serving in office almost doubled the wealth of Conservative MPs but had no discernible financial benefits for Labour MPs. Conservatives profited largely through lucrative outside employment acquired as a result of political positions. This paper pioneered the close-election RDD approach to studying politician wealth.

- **Identification:** RDD on close elections + matching
- **Data:** Estate records of deceased British politicians
- **Main result:** Conservative MPs' wealth nearly doubled vs. close losers (+, very large)
- **Key for us:** Foundational paper for RDD + politician wealth; shows wealth accumulation varies by ideology

### 5. Folke, Marten, Rickne, and Dahlberg (2024) -- "Politicians' Neighborhoods: Where Do They Live, and Does It Matter?"
**Journal:** Journal of Politics, 86(3).
**Relevance:** HIGH | **Proximity:** 5

Politicians live in neighborhoods with more socioeconomically advantaged residents. Using close elections, the authors find that having politicians with governing power in a neighborhood reduces approved building permits for multifamily homes, but not single-family homes. This is the most direct evidence that politicians' place-based interests (property values, neighborhood composition) shape local policy outcomes. Extremely close to our question about geographically tied wealth and policy.

- **Identification:** Close elections RDD, comparing neighborhoods of politicians with varying governing power
- **Data:** Swedish municipal politicians, geocoded addresses, building permit data
- **Main result:** More powerful politicians reduce multifamily building permits in own neighborhoods (-, moderate)
- **Key for us:** DIRECTLY relevant -- shows politicians protect their own neighborhood/property interests

### 6. Christensen and Garfias (2021) -- "The Politics of Property Taxation: Fiscal Infrastructure and Electoral Incentives in Brazil"
**Journal:** Journal of Politics, 83(4), 1399-1416.
**Relevance:** HIGH | **Proximity:** 5

Property tax (IPTU) revenue increases by over 10% following cadastre updates in Brazilian municipalities. However, investing in the cadastre generates political costs by angering tax-averse voters. Officials not facing reelection pressures have greater incentives to invest in the cadastre. Directly relevant to our setting: Brazilian municipal property taxation and the political economy of IPTU collection.

- **Identification:** Panel data with municipal and year FE; electoral cycles
- **Data:** Brazilian municipal fiscal data, cadastre investment data
- **Main result:** Cadastre investment +10% IPTU revenue; term-limited mayors invest more (+, moderate)
- **Key for us:** DIRECTLY relevant -- same context (Brazilian IPTU), same outcome (property tax revenue), different mechanism (electoral incentives vs. personal wealth)

### 7. Hollenbach and Silva (2019) -- "Fiscal Capacity and Inequality: Evidence from Brazilian Municipalities"
**Journal:** Journal of Politics, 81(4), 1434-1445.
**Relevance:** HIGH | **Proximity:** 4

Wealthy elites in democracies can limit their taxes by constraining the fiscal capacity of the state. Using data from over 5,500 Brazilian municipalities, localities with higher inequality accrue less revenue from local property taxes. High-inequality municipalities are also less likely to apply to federal grant programs to increase tax collection capacity. This argues that local elites -- potentially including wealthy mayors -- actively suppress property tax collection.

- **Identification:** Cross-section and panel with municipal and time FE
- **Data:** Brazilian municipal fiscal data, inequality measures, IPTU revenue
- **Main result:** Higher inequality -> lower property tax revenue (-, moderate)
- **Key for us:** Directly relevant -- same outcome (IPTU in Brazil), complementary mechanism (elite capture vs. politician self-interest)

- **Key for us:** Positions our paper within a well-defined literature; identifies gaps we fill

### 9. Tahoun and van Lent (2019) -- "The Personal Wealth Interests of Politicians and Government Intervention in the Economy"
**Journal:** Review of Finance, 23(1), 37-74.
**Relevance:** HIGH | **Proximity:** 4

Politicians with personal financial exposure to the 2008 crisis were almost 60% more likely to vote for the bailout plan than politicians whose wealth was immune. Demonstrates that personal wealth interests directly influence policy votes, even controlling for ideology and electoral incentives. The mechanism is precisely "skin in the game" -- the channel we hypothesize.

- **Identification:** Cross-sectional comparison with controls for ideology and constituency
- **Data:** U.S. Congress members' financial disclosures, TARP votes
- **Main result:** Financial exposure -> 60% more likely to vote for bailout (+, very large)
- **Key for us:** Establishes the core mechanism (personal wealth -> policy) at the national level; we test it at the local level

### 12. Prakash, Rockmore, and Uppal (2019) -- "Do Criminally Accused Politicians Affect Economic Outcomes? Evidence from India"

- **Main result:** Criminally accused politicians lower local growth by ~2.4 pp (-, large)

## Category 2: Brazilian Municipal Politics and Fiscal Policy

### 13. Ferraz and Finan (2008) -- "Exposing Corrupt Politicians: The Effects of Brazil's Publicly Released Audits on Electoral Outcomes"
**Journal:** Quarterly Journal of Economics, 123(2), 703-745.
**Relevance:** HIGH | **Proximity:** 3

Brazil's federal government randomly selects municipalities for audit of federal fund expenditures. The audit policy reduced incumbents' reelection probability by ~20%, more so in municipalities with radio stations. Establishes the institutional context of Brazilian municipal governance, the audit mechanism, and the data infrastructure (CGU audits) relevant to our study.

- **Identification:** Random audit assignment; comparison of pre/post-election audits
- **Data:** CGU audit reports, Brazilian municipal elections
- **Main result:** Audit disclosure reduces reelection by ~20 pp (-, large)
- **Key for us:** Foundational paper on Brazilian municipal accountability; establishes data sources

**Journal:** American Economic Review, 101(4), 1274-1311.
**Relevance:** HIGH | **Proximity:** 3

Mayors with reelection incentives misappropriate 27% fewer resources than term-limited mayors. Effects are more pronounced where information access is limited and judicial punishment is less likely. Establishes that Brazilian mayors respond to electoral incentives in their fiscal behavior -- a key institutional feature for our identification.

- **Identification:** Comparison of term-limited vs. reelectable mayors; CGU random audits
- **Data:** CGU audit data, Brazilian municipal elections
- **Main result:** Reelectable mayors misappropriate 27% less (-, large)
- **Key for us:** Establishes that Brazilian mayors' fiscal behavior is responsive to incentives

## Category 3: Political Selection and Politician Characteristics

### 21. Dal Bo, Finan, Folke, Persson, and Rickne (2017) -- "Who Becomes a Politician?"
**Journal:** Quarterly Journal of Economics, 132(4), 1877-1914.
**Relevance:** HIGH | **Proximity:** 3

Uses extraordinarily rich Swedish administrative data to document strong positive selection into politics: politicians have substantially higher pre-office incomes and ability measures than the general population. This contradicts simple comparative-advantage models predicting negative selection. Relevant because our wealthy-mayor question intersects with political selection: who enters, and does pre-existing wealth matter?

- **Identification:** Descriptive; population-wide administrative data
- **Data:** Swedish population registers, all municipal politicians and national legislators
- **Main result:** Strong positive selection on ability and income into politics (+, large)
- **Key for us:** Establishes that wealthier/more able individuals select into politics; essential for understanding selection concerns

### 22. Besley (2004) -- "Paying Politicians: Theory and Evidence"
**Journal:** Journal of the European Economic Association, 2(2-3), 193-215.
**Relevance:** MEDIUM | **Proximity:** 2

Political agency model with adverse selection and moral hazard. Higher pay attracts better candidates (selection) and improves incentives (moral hazard). Uses data on U.S. governors. This is the theoretical foundation for thinking about how politician compensation (and by extension, wealth) interacts with governance quality.

- **Identification:** Theoretical model + cross-state regressions
- **Data:** U.S. governors' salaries and outcomes
- **Main result:** Higher pay -> better candidates, better outcomes (+, moderate)
- **Key for us:** Theoretical framework linking politician financial incentives to selection and performance

### 23. Gagliarducci and Nannicini (2013) -- "Do Better Paid Politicians Perform Better? Disentangling Incentives from Selection"
**Journal:** Journal of the European Economic Association, 11(2), 369-398.
**Relevance:** MEDIUM | **Proximity:** 3

In Italian municipalities, a higher wage (exploiting a 5,000-inhabitant threshold via RDD) attracts more educated candidates who size down government by improving internal efficiency. Most of the performance effect is driven by selection of competent politicians, not by reelection incentives. Directly analogous method (population-threshold RDD) applied to politician financial characteristics.

- **Identification:** RDD on 5,000-inhabitant threshold for mayor wages
- **Data:** Italian municipal governments, 1993-2001
- **Main result:** Higher wage -> more educated candidates, smaller government machinery (+, moderate)
- **Key for us:** Same method (population-threshold RDD); shows financial incentives shape politician pool and performance

### 38. Calonico, Cattaneo, and Titiunik (2014) -- "Robust Nonparametric Confidence Intervals for Regression-Discontinuity Designs"
**Journal:** Econometrica, 82(6), 2295-2326.

## Category 9: Tax Competition and Strategic Interaction

### 49. Wilson (1986) -- "A Theory of Interregional Tax Competition"
**Journal:** Journal of Urban Economics, 19(3), 296-315.
**Relevance:** MEDIUM | **Proximity:** 2

Wilson (1986) develops a formal model of tax competition among jurisdictions that independently set tax rates on mobile capital to finance local public goods. Each jurisdiction is small relative to the national capital market and takes the net-of-tax return on capital as given. The key result is that non-cooperative tax-setting leads to inefficiently low tax rates and underprovision of local public goods, because each jurisdiction ignores the positive fiscal externality its tax increase confers on other jurisdictions.

- **Identification:** Theoretical model (multi-jurisdiction Nash equilibrium)
- **Data:** N/A (pure theory)
- **Main result:** Tax rates on mobile capital are inefficiently low in Nash equilibrium (-, theoretical)
- **Key for us:** Provides theoretical foundation for asking whether a mayor who suppresses IPTU triggers strategic responses from neighboring municipalities

### 50. Zodrow and Mieszkowski (1986) -- "Pigou, Tiebout, Property Taxation, and the Underprovision of Local Public Goods"
**Journal:** Journal of Urban Economics, 19(3), 356-370.
**Relevance:** MEDIUM | **Proximity:** 3

Published in the same JUE issue as Wilson (1986), the "ZM model" shows that interjurisdictional competition for mobile capital leads to property tax rates that are too low and public good provision that is too little relative to the social optimum. The paper specifically addresses property taxation and the Tiebout hypothesis, showing that even when households sort efficiently, the property tax generates a fiscal externality. This establishes the "new view" of property taxation: local property taxes function as distortionary taxes on mobile capital rather than benefit fees.

- **Identification:** Theoretical model (general equilibrium with mobile capital)
- **Data:** N/A (pure theory)
- **Main result:** Property taxes are set inefficiently low under interjurisdictional competition (-, theoretical)
- **Key for us:** Most directly relevant foundational theory -- concerns property taxation specifically (IPTU). If wealthy mayors suppress IPTU further below already-suboptimal competitive levels, the welfare cost is compounded

### 51. Keen and Konrad (2013) -- "The Theory of International Tax Competition and Coordination"
**Book chapter:** Handbook of Public Economics, Vol. 5, pp. 257-328.
**Relevance:** MEDIUM | **Proximity:** 2

Definitive survey of three decades of tax competition research. Covers the basic model and extensions (asymmetric jurisdictions, multiple instruments, agglomeration), political economy of tax competition (yardstick competition, Leviathan models), and empirical evidence. Key empirical findings: strong evidence of strategic interaction (tax reaction function slopes of 0.3-0.7), mixed evidence on "race to the bottom" in levels, and evidence that smaller jurisdictions set lower rates. Discusses the political economy intersection where our paper sits.

- **Identification:** Survey (synthesizes empirical findings from spatial econometrics, IV, and natural experiments)
- **Data:** N/A (survey)
- **Main result:** Robust theoretical prediction of "too low" taxes under competition; empirical strategic interaction slopes 0.3-0.7
- **Key for us:** Essential reference for positioning in the broader tax competition literature. Signals to JPubE/JUE referees that we engage with the theoretical context

### 52. Agrawal (2015) -- "The Tax Gradient: Spatial Aspects of Fiscal Competition"
**Journal:** American Economic Journal: Economic Policy, 7(2), 1-29.
**Relevance:** MEDIUM | **Proximity:** 3

Estimates how strategic tax interaction decays with geographic distance using local sales tax rates across U.S. jurisdictions near the Kansas-Missouri border. Finds strong positive strategic interaction: a 1 pp increase in neighbor's rate leads to ~0.4-0.6 pp own-rate increase for immediately adjacent jurisdictions, declining rapidly with distance. Also finds that entry of a new taxing jurisdiction lowers neighboring jurisdictions' rates.

- **Identification:** Spatial econometrics with IV (instruments: neighbor demographic characteristics); Kansas-Missouri border as variation source
- **Data:** Jurisdiction-level sales tax rates, Kansas and Missouri, 2002-2011
- **Main result:** Positive strategic complementarity; own-rate elasticity ~0.4-0.6 for immediate neighbors, declining with distance (+, moderate)
- **Key for us:** Provides the empirical methodology for testing whether wealthy mayors' IPTU decisions generate spatial spillovers to neighboring Brazilian municipalities

### 53. Parchet (2019) -- "Are Local Tax Rates Strategic Complements or Strategic Substitutes?"
**Journal:** American Economic Journal: Economic Policy, 11(2), 189-224.
**Relevance:** MEDIUM | **Proximity:** 3

Studies tax competition among Swiss municipalities using IV based on cantonal fiscal equalization reforms. Finds that naive spatial regressions are badly biased: OLS shows strategic complementarity (+0.3 to +0.5) but IV reveals strategic substitutability (-0.5 to -0.7). The mechanism is income sorting (Tiebout mobility) -- when a neighbor cuts taxes, high-income households move there, forcing the own jurisdiction to raise rates to maintain revenue.

- **Identification:** IV exploiting cantonal fiscal equalization reforms as shifters of neighbor incentives
- **Data:** Swiss municipality-level income tax rates, 1983-2012
- **Main result:** Strategic substitutability: neighbor rate cuts lead to own-rate increases (-, moderate). OLS bias reverses the sign
- **Key for us:** Critical methodological warning for our spatial analysis. Connects to Tiebout/sorting concern. For Brazil, where inter-municipal mobility is lower than Switzerland, sorting effects may be weaker

## Category 10: Tax Capitalization and Property Values

### 54. Oates (1969) -- "The Effects of Property Taxes and Local Public Spending on Property Values"
**Journal:** Journal of Political Economy, 77(6), 957-971.
**Relevance:** MEDIUM | **Proximity:** 2

First systematic empirical test of property tax capitalization. Using cross-sectional data from 53 New Jersey municipalities, Oates showed that higher property tax rates are associated with lower property values and higher school expenditures with higher values, consistent with the Tiebout hypothesis. Estimated magnitudes broadly consistent with full capitalization at prevailing discount rates. Foundational paper establishing why a mayor with local real estate should care about local tax policy.

- **Identification:** Cross-sectional OLS (property values on tax rates and expenditures, controlling for community characteristics)
- **Data:** 1960 U.S. Census, 53 municipalities in the NJ portion of NYC metro area
- **Main result:** Higher taxes -> lower property values, roughly full capitalization (-, moderate-large)
- **Key for us:** Establishes the economic mechanism underlying "skin in the game": if IPTU capitalizes into property values, locally-wealthy mayors have dollar-for-dollar stakes in tax policy

### 55. Brueckner (1979) -- "Property Values, Local Public Expenditure and Economic Efficiency"
**Journal:** Journal of Public Economics, 11(2), 223-245.
**Relevance:** MEDIUM | **Proximity:** 2

Developed the theoretical link between property value maximization by local governments and efficient public goods provision. - **Data:** 1970 U.S. Census, 54 communities in the Boston SMSA
- **Main result:** Property values follow inverted-U with public expenditures, peaking near observed levels (+, moderate)

### 56. Palmon and Smith (1998) -- "New Evidence on Property Tax Capitalization"
**Journal:** Journal of Political Economy, 106(5), 1099-1111.

## Frontier Map

### What Is Established

1. **Politician characteristics causally affect policy.** A large body of evidence using close-election RDD shows that who governs matters: gender (Chattopadhyay and Duflo 2004; Brollo and Troiano 2016), education (Besley, Montalvo, and Reynal-Querol 2011), criminal background (Prakash et al. 2019), business experience (Szakonyi 2018, 2020), and party affiliation (various) all causally shape policy and economic outcomes.

2. **Politicians derive private financial returns from office.** Fisman et al. (2014) show winners' assets grow 3-5 pp/year faster than losers in India. Eggers and Hainmueller (2009) show Conservative MPs doubled their wealth. Szakonyi (2018) shows connected firms increase revenue by 60% in Russia. The direction is clear: holding office is financially beneficial.

3. **Politicians' personal financial interests influence policy votes.** Tahoun and van Lent (2019) show a direct link between U.S. congresspeople's financial exposure and their bailout votes. Folke et al. (2024) show politicians with governing power reduce building permits in their own neighborhoods.

4. **Brazilian municipal politics is a well-studied laboratory.** Close-election RDD has been extensively validated in Brazilian mayoral races (Ferraz and Finan 2008, 2011; Brollo et al. 2013; Brollo and Troiano 2016; Colonnelli et al. 2020). The data infrastructure (TSE electoral data, CGU audits, RAIS labor records, FINBRA fiscal data) is mature and publicly available.

5. **Property taxation in Brazil is politically determined.** Christensen and Garfias (2021) show cadastre investment depends on electoral incentives. Hollenbach and Silva (2019) show local inequality suppresses property tax collection. The under-collection of IPTU is well-documented (about 0.5% of GDP vs. potential of 0.9-1.2%).

### What Is Debated

1. **Selection vs. incentive mechanisms.** Does positive selection of wealthy politicians improve governance (Dal Bo et al. 2017, Besley 2004), or does it create self-dealing opportunities (Szakonyi 2018)? The Gagliarducci and Nannicini (2013) finding that selection dominates incentives may not hold when politicians have direct financial stakes.

2. **Direction of wealth effect on taxation.** Wealthy mayors could either (a) suppress property taxes to benefit their own real estate (self-dealing), (b) improve fiscal administration because they have better information and management skills (competence), or (c) show no effect because ideology and party discipline dominate. The sign is theoretically ambiguous.

3. **External validity of RDD politician-characteristic estimates.** de Magalhães et al. (2025) and Marshall (2022) question what close-election RDD estimates capture when applied to politician characteristics. The LATE is for a very specific complier population (marginal election outcomes).

### What Is the Gap -- Where Our Paper Fits

**The central gap:** No paper examines whether the *geographic concentration* of a politician's personal wealth in their own jurisdiction affects fiscal policy. Existing work either:
- Studies private returns TO office (Fisman et al. 2014; Eggers and Hainmueller 2009) -- the reverse direction
- Studies business politicians' policy effects without distinguishing local vs. non-local assets (Szakonyi 2018, 2020)
- Studies neighborhood effects for legislators without fiscal outcomes (Folke et al. 2024)
- Studies Brazilian property taxation without politician-level variation (Christensen and Garfias 2021; Hollenbach and Silva 2019)

**Our paper bridges these literatures.** We combine:
- The *politician wealth* literature (Fisman et al. 2014, Szakonyi 2018) with
- The *Brazilian municipal fiscal policy* literature (Christensen and Garfias 2021, Hollenbach and Silva 2019) and
- The *close-election RDD* methodology validated in Brazilian mayoral races (Brollo and Troiano 2016, Colonnelli et al. 2020)

The novelty is the *geographic concentration* dimension: a mayor whose wealth is entirely in local real estate has different fiscal incentives than a mayor whose wealth is in financial assets or real estate elsewhere. The TSE asset declarations allow us to classify wealth by type and (with geocoding) by location.

## Positioning Recommendation

### Suggested Contribution Statement

"This paper is the first to study whether the geographic concentration of politicians' personal wealth in their own jurisdiction affects local fiscal policy. Using mandatory asset declarations from Brazilian mayoral candidates and a regression discontinuity design exploiting close elections, we show that mayors with wealth concentrated in their municipality [set lower/higher property tax rates | collect less/more IPTU revenue | invest differently in fiscal infrastructure] relative to mayors whose wealth is geographically dispersed. These findings speak to a 'skin in the game' mechanism in local governance: when politicians' personal assets are directly affected by the policies they set, fiscal outcomes change."

### Key Mechanisms to Emphasize

1. **Skin-in-the-game (positive):** Mayors with local wealth may govern better because policy consequences directly affect them
2. **Self-dealing (negative):** Mayors may suppress property taxes or direct spending to benefit own assets
3. **Information (positive):** Local wealth implies local knowledge about tax base and economic conditions
4. **Selection (neutral):** Wealthier candidates who invest locally may differ on unobservable dimensions -- this is a concern, not a mechanism

The paper should present theoretical predictions from each mechanism and test for which dominates empirically.
