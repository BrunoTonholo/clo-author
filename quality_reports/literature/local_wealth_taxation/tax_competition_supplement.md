## Paper 1: Wilson (1986) -- Foundational Model of Tax Competition

### Full Citation

Wilson, John Douglas. "A Theory of Interregional Tax Competition." *Journal of Urban Economics*, Vol. 19, No. 3, pp. 296-315, 1986.

### Summary

Wilson (1986) develops a formal model of tax competition among jurisdictions that independently set tax rates on mobile capital to finance local public goods. In the model, each jurisdiction is small relative to the national capital market and takes the net-of-tax return on capital as given. The key result is that non-cooperative tax-setting leads to inefficiently low tax rates and underprovision of local public goods, because each jurisdiction ignores the positive fiscal externality its tax increase confers on other jurisdictions (by pushing capital there). The paper establishes the canonical "race to the bottom" prediction that became the benchmark for all subsequent tax competition research.

### Identification Strategy

Theoretical paper -- no empirical identification. Derives equilibrium in a multi-jurisdiction model with perfectly mobile capital and immobile residents, comparing Nash equilibrium tax rates to the social optimum.

### Key Data Source

N/A (pure theory).

### Main Result

Tax rates on mobile capital are inefficiently low in Nash equilibrium. The underprovision of public goods increases with the number of competing jurisdictions and the mobility of capital. Sign: negative (equilibrium taxes below optimum). Magnitude: depends on parameters (number of jurisdictions, capital mobility elasticity).

### Relevance to Our Project (Proximity: 2)

This paper provides the theoretical foundation for asking whether a mayor who suppresses local property taxes (IPTU) triggers strategic responses from neighboring municipalities. If wealthy mayors set lower IPTU to protect their own real estate wealth, the Wilson framework predicts neighboring jurisdictions may also lower rates (strategic complementarity) or attract mobile tax base. The model motivates testing for spatial spillovers: does a wealthy mayor's tax suppression propagate through tax competition? This is essential theoretical background for any referee at JPubE, JUE, or AEJ:EP who sees our local taxation paper.

## Paper 2: Zodrow and Mieszkowski (1986) -- The ZM Model

### Full Citation

Zodrow, George R. and Mieszkowski, Peter. "Pigou, Tiebout, Property Taxation, and the Underprovision of Local Public Goods." *Journal of Urban Economics*, Vol. 19, No. 3, pp. 356-370, 1986.

### Summary

Published in the same issue of JUE as Wilson (1986), Zodrow and Mieszkowski (1986) develop a closely related model -- sometimes called the "ZM model" -- showing that interjurisdictional competition for mobile capital leads to property tax rates that are too low and public good provision that is too little relative to the social optimum. Their framework specifically addresses the Tiebout hypothesis by showing that even when households sort efficiently across jurisdictions, the property tax still generates a fiscal externality: a jurisdiction raising its tax drives capital to other jurisdictions, benefiting them. This contradicts the Tiebout view that decentralized provision is efficient. The result that property taxes are "too low" under competition (the opposite of Pigouvian over-taxation) became known as the "new view" of property taxation, in contrast to the "old view" (Harberger tradition) and the "benefit view" (Tiebout tradition).

### Identification Strategy

Theoretical paper -- no empirical identification. Uses a general equilibrium model with identical jurisdictions, mobile capital, immobile land and labor, and a property tax on capital.

### Key Data Source

N/A (pure theory).

### Main Result

Property taxes are set inefficiently low in a decentralized equilibrium with competing jurisdictions. The "new view" of property taxation: local property taxes function as distortionary taxes on mobile capital rather than benefit fees, leading to underprovision. Sign: negative (taxes below optimum). This directly contradicts the Tiebout efficiency result.

### Relevance to Our Project (Proximity: 3)

This is the most directly relevant foundational theory paper for our project because it concerns property taxation specifically -- exactly the IPTU outcome we study. The ZM model predicts that competitive pressures already push property taxes below optimally efficient levels. Our contribution can be framed as identifying a novel channel (mayor's personal wealth) that exacerbates or mitigates this pre-existing underprovision. If wealthy mayors suppress IPTU further below already-suboptimal competitive levels, the welfare cost is compounded. If wealthy mayors with local stakes actually increase IPTU (skin-in-the-game), they may partially correct the ZM underprovision problem. The paper also provides the conceptual distinction between "old view" (property tax as excise tax on housing), "new view" (property tax as distortionary capital tax), and "benefit view" (property tax as user charge) that any referee will expect us to engage with.

## Paper 3: Keen and Konrad (2013) -- Comprehensive Survey

### Full Citation

Keen, Michael and Konrad, Kai A. "The Theory of International Tax Competition and Coordination." In Alan J. Auerbach, Raj Chetty, Martin Feldstein, and Emmanuel Saez (eds.), *Handbook of Public Economics*, Vol. 5, pp. 257-328. Amsterdam: Elsevier (North-Holland), 2013.

### Summary

Keen and Konrad (2013) provide the definitive survey of the tax competition literature as of its publication, covering three decades of theoretical and empirical developments since Wilson (1986) and Zodrow-Mieszkowski (1986). The chapter organizes the literature into several strands: the basic tax competition model and its extensions (asymmetric jurisdictions, multiple tax instruments, agglomeration), tax competition with imperfect factor mobility, political economy of tax competition (yardstick competition, Leviathan models), empirical evidence on strategic tax interaction, and tax coordination (minimum rates, harmonization). Key empirical findings summarized include: (1) strong evidence of strategic interaction in tax-setting (tax reaction functions with positive slopes), (2) mixed evidence on "race to the bottom" in levels, (3) evidence that smaller jurisdictions set lower rates (consistent with theory), and (4) nascent evidence on the welfare costs of competition. The chapter concludes that while the basic theoretical prediction of inefficiently low taxes is robust, the real-world picture is complicated by political economy considerations, imperfect mobility, and heterogeneous jurisdictions.

### Identification Strategy

Survey chapter -- synthesizes empirical findings from multiple studies. The empirical papers surveyed typically use spatial econometrics (tax reaction functions estimated via spatial lag models), instrumental variables for neighbor tax rates, and natural experiments (e.g., border changes, EU integration).

### Key Data Source

N/A (survey). Summarizes evidence from OECD corporate tax data, European local government data, U.S. state/local tax data, and developing country municipal data.

### Main Result

The theoretical prediction of "too low" taxes under competition is robust across model variants. Empirically, there is strong evidence of strategic interaction (positive reaction function slopes, typically 0.3-0.7) but the welfare costs of competition are hard to quantify. The "race to the bottom" in levels is less clear-cut than the theory predicts, partly because political economy forces (politicians may over-tax if Leviathan, or under-tax if competing for mobile capital) create offsetting effects.

### Relevance to Our Project (Proximity: 2)

This survey is the essential reference for positioning our paper in the broader tax competition literature. Three specific connections: (1) The "political economy of tax competition" section discusses how politician characteristics and institutional design interact with competitive pressures -- our paper adds a new dimension (mayor's personal wealth). (2) The empirical evidence on tax reaction functions motivates testing whether the effect of mayor wealth on IPTU propagates to neighboring municipalities -- a spatial spillover analysis. (3) The chapter's discussion of developing country tax competition is sparse, identifying a gap that our Brazilian evidence can help fill. Citing this survey signals to referees that we are aware of the broader theoretical context beyond the Brazil-specific political economy literature.

## Paper 4: Agrawal (2015) -- Empirical Tax Competition with Local Sales Taxes

### Full Citation

Agrawal, David R. "The Tax Gradient: Spatial Aspects of Fiscal Competition." *American Economic Journal: Economic Policy*, Vol. 7, No. 2, pp. 1-29, 2015.

### Summary

Agrawal (2015) provides one of the cleanest empirical studies of local tax competition by exploiting the border between Kansas and Missouri and the variation in local sales tax rates across jurisdictions. Using detailed jurisdiction-level tax rate data, the paper estimates how a jurisdiction's tax rate responds to the rates set by neighboring jurisdictions at different distances. The key innovation is estimating a "tax gradient" -- how the intensity of strategic interaction decays with geographic distance. The paper finds strong evidence of positive strategic interaction: a one percentage point increase in a neighbor's sales tax rate leads to approximately a 0.4-0.6 percentage point increase in own rates. Crucially, this reaction is strongest for immediately adjacent jurisdictions and declines rapidly with distance, consistent with competition for a geographically limited mobile tax base (shoppers who cross borders). The paper also finds that the entry of a new taxing jurisdiction lowers neighboring jurisdictions' rates.

### Identification Strategy

Spatial econometrics with instrumental variables. Uses a spatial lag model where a jurisdiction's tax rate depends on the weighted average of neighbors' rates. Instruments for neighbors' rates using their demographic characteristics (population, income) that predict tax rates but are plausibly exogenous to own rate-setting. Also exploits the Kansas-Missouri state border as a source of variation in the tax competition environment (different state-level tax structures create differential incentives for local competition).

### Key Data Source

Jurisdiction-level sales tax rates for all municipalities and counties in Kansas and Missouri (2002-2011), from state departments of revenue. Geographic boundary files for computing distances and adjacency.

### Main Result

Positive strategic complementarity: own-rate elasticity with respect to neighbor rates is approximately 0.4-0.6 for immediate neighbors, declining with distance. A new taxing jurisdiction within 2 miles reduces neighbors' rates by 0.1-0.2 percentage points. The tax gradient decays substantially beyond 5-10 miles.

### Relevance to Our Project (Proximity: 3)

This paper provides the empirical methodology template for testing whether wealthy mayors' tax policy choices generate spatial spillovers to neighboring Brazilian municipalities. If a wealthy mayor suppresses IPTU, does the neighboring municipality respond? Agrawal's "tax gradient" approach -- estimating strategic interaction as a function of geographic distance -- is directly applicable. For Brazil, we can estimate whether the RDD-identified effect of a wealthy mayor on own-municipality IPTU spills over to neighbors. The U.S. sales tax setting differs from Brazilian property taxes, but the spatial econometric framework (tax reaction functions, IV for neighbor rates) transfers directly. This is a strong methodological precedent for an empirical tax competition extension of our paper.

## Paper 5: Parchet (2019) -- Tax Competition and Income Sorting

### Full Citation

Parchet, Raphael. "Are Local Tax Rates Strategic Complements or Strategic Substitutes?" *American Economic Journal: Economic Policy*, Vol. 11, No. 2, pp. 189-224, 2019.

### Summary

Parchet (2019) studies tax competition among Swiss municipalities, addressing a fundamental identification challenge: observed positive correlations in neighboring jurisdictions' tax rates could reflect either genuine strategic interaction (strategic complements) or correlated shocks. Using a novel instrumental variable strategy based on cantons' historical fiscal equalization reforms -- which shifted municipalities' incentive to set tax rates without directly affecting neighbors -- the paper finds that the sign of strategic interaction reverses once endogeneity is addressed. Naive spatial lag regressions suggest strategic complementarity (positive reaction function slopes), but the IV estimates reveal strategic substitutability: when a neighbor lowers its income tax rate, the own jurisdiction raises its rate. The mechanism is income sorting: when a neighbor cuts taxes, high-income households move there, eroding the own jurisdiction's tax base and forcing it to raise rates to maintain revenue. This finding challenges the standard Wilson/ZM prediction of a "race to the bottom" and suggests that sorting can overturn the canonical result.

### Identification Strategy

Instrumental variables exploiting plausibly exogenous variation from cantonal fiscal equalization reforms in Switzerland. Reforms changed the effective cost of public goods provision for municipalities in the reforming canton (by altering equalization transfers), shifting their incentive to set tax rates, without directly affecting the tax-setting incentives of neighboring municipalities in other cantons. Two-stage least squares with the reform-induced shift as an instrument for neighbor rates.

### Key Data Source

Municipality-level income tax rates and tax base data for all Swiss municipalities (1983-2012), from the Swiss Federal Tax Administration. Cantonal fiscal equalization reform dates and parameters.

### Main Result

Strategic substitutability, not complementarity: IV estimates show a negative reaction function slope of approximately -0.5 to -0.7 (a one percentage point cut by neighbors leads to a 0.5-0.7 pp increase in own rate). This contrasts with naive OLS estimates showing positive slopes of +0.3 to +0.5. The mechanism is income sorting (Tiebout mobility) rather than capital competition.

### Relevance to Our Project (Proximity: 3)

This paper is directly relevant because it raises a critical empirical question about how to interpret spatial tax spillovers in our setting. If a wealthy Brazilian mayor suppresses IPTU and neighboring municipalities raise their rates in response (strategic substitutes due to tax base sorting), the welfare implications differ fundamentally from a "race to the bottom" scenario (strategic complements). Parchet's finding that naive spatial regressions are badly biased -- reversing sign -- is a methodological warning for our spatial analysis. The paper also connects to our project's "Tiebout/sorting" concern flagged by the Editor: if households sort in response to IPTU differentials created by wealthy mayors, the capitalization and migration channels are empirically important. For Brazil, where residential mobility between municipalities is lower than in Switzerland, we might expect weaker sorting responses, but this is an empirical question.

## How These Papers Connect to Our Project

### The Tax Competition Channel in Our Paper

Our core question is whether mayors' locally concentrated wealth affects IPTU policy. The tax competition literature adds a critical dimension:

1. **Baseline distortion (ZM):** Even without wealthy mayors, property taxes may already be inefficiently low due to interjurisdictional competition. Our paper can test whether mayor wealth exacerbates or mitigates this pre-existing distortion.

2. **Spatial spillovers (Wilson, Agrawal):** If a wealthy mayor suppresses IPTU, do neighboring municipalities respond? Estimating tax reaction functions around the RDD cutoff would add a spatial dimension to our analysis.

3. **Direction of strategic interaction (Parchet):** Whether neighbors respond with complements (lowering own rates, race to bottom) or substitutes (raising own rates due to tax base sorting) has different welfare implications and is an empirical question for Brazil.

4. **Referee expectations (Keen and Konrad):** Any referee at JPubE, JUE, or AEJ:EP will expect engagement with this literature. Citing these papers signals awareness of the broader context beyond politician-characteristics and Brazilian political economy.

### Suggested Integration Points in the Paper

- **Introduction:** One paragraph noting that local property taxation is shaped by both political selection (our contribution) and interjurisdictional competition (established literature). Frame our contribution as identifying a new determinant of tax policy within the existing competitive environment.
- **Literature review:** A dedicated subsection titled "Tax Competition and Local Public Finance" placing Wilson/ZM/Keen-Konrad as theoretical background and Agrawal/Parchet as empirical precedents.
- **Extensions/robustness:** A spatial spillover test estimating whether wealthy-mayor municipalities' IPTU changes propagate to neighbors. This could be framed as a "tax competition extension" that strengthens the paper.
- **Mechanisms discussion:** The sorting channel (Parchet) connects to the capitalization concern -- if IPTU suppression attracts new residents, property values change, affecting the wealthy mayor's own wealth endogenously.
